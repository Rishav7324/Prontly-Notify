Executive Summary
Prontly Notify is a multi-tenant SaaS platform that lets website owners collect, segment, and message browser-push subscribers — positioning it as a direct, India-headquartered alternative to OneSignal, PushEngage, and Webpushr, purpose-built for bloggers, news publishers, AI tool builders, SaaS founders, e-commerce stores, and agencies who currently rely on email and social alone for re-engagement.
The platform's core wedge against the existing field is depth of AI tooling at every tier — not just send tools, but a generation, optimization, scheduling, and reporting layer built directly into the campaign workflow — combined with an edge-first technical stack (Cloudflare D1 + R2, Vercel, Next.js 15) that keeps infrastructure cost low enough to offer a genuinely usable free tier, the single biggest activation lever in this category.

Business Context
Website owners across every target persona lose the majority of their visitors after a single session, with no low-cost, high-reach way to bring them back — email requires an address most visitors never give, retargeting ads are expensive and degrading, and organic social reach keeps shrinking. Browser push is the only zero-install, permission-based channel that still reaches a returning visitor directly. Prontly Notify exists to make that channel usable by the long tail of website owners currently priced out or engineering-blocked from raw Firebase Cloud Messaging.
Stack Justification
Next.js 15 + Cloudflare D1/R2 + Vercel was chosen specifically because it lets a small team ship a genuinely usable free tier without the infrastructure cost structure of a traditional Postgres-plus-S3 stack — the free tier is the product's primary growth lever, so keeping its marginal cost near zero is a business decision as much as a technical one. Firebase Authentication and Firebase Cloud Messaging share one underlying provider relationship, simplifying both the auth and delivery integration surface. Razorpay was chosen for its strength in the India-primary market while still supporting the secondary US/UK/Canada/Australia markets.

Product Requirements Document (PRD)
1. Executive Summary
Prontly Notify is a multi-tenant SaaS platform that gives any website owner a OneSignal-class browser push notification engine — subscriber collection, campaign composition, behavioral automation, audience segmentation, and analytics — wrapped in an AI layer that writes, times, targets, and explains campaigns on the user's behalf. It exists now because the re-engagement channel landscape has hardened: email open rates keep falling, organic social reach keeps shrinking, and browser push remains the only zero-permission-fatigue, app-install-free channel that delivers a message directly to a returning visitor's device. The market leaders (OneSignal, PushEngage) have not meaningfully invested in AI-native workflows, and none offer an edge-deployed, India-priced alternative built for the long tail of bloggers, AI-tool builders, and small e-commerce stores who are priced out of enterprise engagement suites.
2. Problem Statement
Core user pain: Website owners lose the majority of their visitors after a single session and have no reliable, low-cost way to bring them back. Email requires an address most visitors never give up; retargeting ads are expensive and degrading as third-party cookies disappear; social algorithms bury organic posts.
Current alternatives and shortcomings: 
* OneSignal — powerful but enterprise-priced at scale, generic (not push-notification-only) UI, no AI campaign assistance, support is slow for self-serve customers.
* PushEngage / Webpushr — push-focused but dated UI, weak segmentation tooling, automation gated behind expensive plans.
* Raw Firebase Cloud Messaging — free and powerful but requires the website owner to build their own dashboard, subscriber storage, campaign UI, and analytics from scratch — a multi-week engineering project most target users cannot undertake.
Why this solution is better: Prontly Notify combines FCM's reliability with a purpose-built, AI-assisted campaign workflow and a genuinely usable free tier made possible by a low-cost edge stack (Cloudflare D1/R2 + Vercel), removing both the engineering burden of raw FCM and the cost/complexity ceiling of incumbent SaaS tools.

	8. Risks & Assumptions
Technical Risks
* Risk: FCM web push delivery rates degrade due to browser-vendor policy changes (e.g., Chrome's permission-prompt friction features). Mitigation: monitor delivery-rate dashboards (Section: Delivery Monitoring) and maintain an abstraction layer so an alternate push provider could be added without a full rewrite.
* Risk: Cloudflare D1's relatively young feature set (e.g., row/database size limits) constrains very large subscriber tables on top accounts. Mitigation: partition subscriber data per-site from day one and monitor D1 documented limits as the platform scales; plan a read-replica/analytics-offload path before any single site approaches table-size ceilings.
Business Risks
* Risk: Free tier is generous enough to suppress upgrade pressure. Mitigation: gate the highest-leverage features (AI credits beyond a small monthly allotment, automation step count, multi-site limits) rather than core sending volume alone.
* Risk: Sender-reputation damage from a single abusive tenant affects deliverability platform-wide. Mitigation: automated spam-content screening plus the admin Website Oversight and Delivery Monitoring tooling specified in this blueprint.
Assumptions To Validate
* Assumption: target personas will tolerate a service-worker-based install process (vs. a one-click app-store install). Validate via onboarding completion-rate tracking in the first 90 days post-launch.
* Assumption: AI-generated titles measurably outperform manually written ones for this audience. Validate via an in-product A/B comparison surfaced in the Analytics AI summary.

	2. Infrastructure Architecture
System overview: The Next.js application is deployed to Vercel as a single project serving three route groups — public marketing/SEO pages (ISR-cached), the authenticated dashboard (client-rendered, API-driven), and the admin panel (role-gated). All data access goes through Next.js API routes acting as a thin service layer in front of Cloudflare D1. A separate scheduled-worker process (Vercel Cron invoking an API route) handles time-based campaign sends, automation step execution, and FCM delivery batching.
Data flow overview: 
1. A subscriber's browser registers a service worker (served from R2/CDN) and obtains an FCM token via the Web Push API.
2. The token, along with browser/OS/geo metadata, is POSTed to /api/v1/sites/:id/subscribers and written to D1.
3. When a user composes a campaign, the API resolves the target segment into a subscriber-token list (paginated for large audiences) and enqueues delivery batches.
4. A Cron-triggered worker calls the FCM HTTP v1 API in batches, records delivery/failure status per token back into D1, and updates the campaign's live analytics counters.
5. The dashboard polls or re-fetches analytics endpoints, which read pre-aggregated counters from D1 rather than scanning raw event tables on every page load.
Third-party integrations: Firebase Cloud Messaging (delivery), Firebase Authentication (identity), Razorpay (billing), Google Analytics 4 (marketing analytics), and an LLM provider via server-side API calls for all AI features (title generation, CTR optimization, scheduling suggestions, campaign recommendations, segmentation suggestions, analytics summaries) — never called client-side, to protect API keys and allow per-plan usage metering.
3. Security Requirements
Authentication & authorization model: Firebase Authentication issues a signed ID token on login; Next.js middleware verifies the token on every protected route and attaches the resolved user + role + active-workspace context to the request. Role-based access control distinguishes Owner, Admin (team), Member (team, scoped), and Prontly Super Admin / Admin (internal staff), enforced both in middleware and again at the database-query layer (defense in depth).
* Encryption at rest: D1 data is encrypted at rest by the underlying Cloudflare infrastructure; R2 objects use server-side encryption by default.
* Encryption in transit: TLS 1.2+ enforced on all Vercel and Cloudflare endpoints; HSTS header set platform-wide.
* API security: every protected route requires a valid Firebase ID token (15-minute-lifetime access pattern via SDK refresh) or a scoped API key for developer-persona REST access; refresh handled transparently by the Firebase client SDK.
* Input validation: all request bodies validated server-side with Zod schemas before touching the database layer; no client-supplied value is trusted for authorization decisions.
* OWASP Top 10 mitigation: parameterized D1 queries (injection), output encoding + CSP (XSS), CSRF tokens on state-changing dashboard forms, dependency scanning in CI (vulnerable components), audit logging + least-privilege role checks (broken access control).

	2. Universal State Model
Every data-driven screen in the product (anything beyond static marketing pages) implements the same four-state model, so AI coding agents and engineers can apply one consistent pattern across the codebase:
State
	Behavior
	Default
	Data loaded successfully; full interactive UI rendered with real content.
	Loading
	Skeleton placeholders matching the eventual content's shape (not a generic spinner) for any fetch over ~150ms; KPI cards, tables, and charts each have a dedicated skeleton variant.
	Empty
	No data exists yet (e.g., zero subscribers, zero campaigns). Shows a purpose-built illustration, a one-sentence explanation, and a single primary CTA toward the action that would populate the screen (e.g., 'Send your first campaign').
	Error
	API failure or permission denial. Shows a clear, non-technical message, a 'Retry' action where applicable, and never a raw stack trace or status code in the user-facing copy.
	Success / Confirmation
	Action completed (campaign sent, settings saved, invite sent). Surfaced via a toast for low-stakes actions and an inline confirmation panel for high-stakes ones (e.g., billing changes, account suspension).

	4. Primary User Journeys
Journey A — New Visitor → Signup → First Value Moment
1. Lands on Home via organic search for 'browser push notification for WordPress'.
2. Scrolls hero, feature grid, and pricing teaser; clicks 'Start Free'.
3. Completes Signup with email/password.
4. Verifies email (polled automatically; no manual refresh needed).
5. Completes the 3-step Website Onboarding Wizard, selecting WordPress as the platform and pasting the provided plugin snippet.
6. Verification step confirms the service worker is live; sees the success animation.
7. Lands on Dashboard Home with zero subscribers (Empty state) and a persistent 'Invite your first subscribers' banner.
8. First Value Moment: the user's own test notification (sent during onboarding) appears in their browser — the 'aha' moment that proves the system works end-to-end.
Journey B — Returning User → Login → Core Action → Exit
1. Logs in via Login screen; lands on Dashboard Home for their last-active website.
2. Reviews the AI insight card recommending a re-engagement campaign for an 'at risk' segment.
3. Clicks through to Campaign Builder with that segment pre-selected.
4. Uses the AI title generator, accepts a suggested title, reviews the live preview, and sends immediately.
5. Redirected to Campaigns History, sees the new campaign at the top with a 'Sent' badge, and closes the tab.
Journey C — Admin → Login → Manage Users → Take Action
1. Internal staff logs in; Firebase custom claim resolves their role as Admin, routing them to /admin instead of /dashboard.
2. Admin Dashboard shows an elevated 'Delivery Success Rate' alert for a specific site.
3. Drills into Website Oversight, filters by the flagged domain, and inspects its recent send history.
4. Determines the site is sending unsolicited promotional spam disguised as content notifications; clicks 'Disable Sending' with a required reason.
5. Action is recorded in Audit Logs; the affected account owner receives an automated notice with an appeal path via Contact.
5. Edge Cases
Edge Case
	Behavior
	Session expiry mid-session
	API calls return 401; the client intercepts this globally, shows a non-disruptive 'Your session expired' modal, and redirects to Login preserving the current route as a post-login redirect target.
	Network failure during a write action (e.g., campaign send)
	The action is not silently retried automatically (to avoid duplicate sends); the UI shows an explicit error with a manual 'Retry' button and the draft is preserved client-side.
	Permission escalation attempt (Member tries to access Billing)
	Server-side role check returns 403 regardless of any client-side route guard; the UI shows a 'You don't have access to this page' state with a link back to Dashboard Home, never a blank screen.
	Permission downgrade mid-session (Owner removes a teammate's access while they're active)
	The next API call from the downgraded session returns 403; the client surfaces a session-refresh prompt that re-evaluates the user's current role.
	Account deletion
	Multi-step confirmation (type the workspace name to confirm) → all sites' sending is immediately disabled → a 30-day soft-delete grace period (recoverable via Contact) → permanent purge of D1 records and R2 assets after the grace period.
	Data export request (GDPR/DPDPA)
	Available from Account Settings → Security tab as 'Export my data'; triggers an async job that compiles account, sites, campaigns (metadata, not full subscriber PII beyond what's needed), and billing history into a downloadable archive, emailed as a signed, expiring R2 link.
	6. Permission Flows
Unauthenticated access to a protected route: Next.js middleware checks for a valid session before the route renders; absence redirects to /login with a `?redirect=` query param so the user lands back where they intended after authenticating.
Non-admin access to /admin/*: Middleware checks the Firebase custom claim for admin role; failure returns a 404 (not a 403) for the /admin path specifically, to avoid revealing the existence of the admin panel to non-staff users probing the URL.

	UI/UX Brief — Design System & Experience Guidelines
1. Design Principles
Principle
	What It Means In Practice
	Confidence over cuteness
	Every screen should feel like infrastructure software (closer to Stripe/Vercel than a consumer app) — generous whitespace, restrained color use, data presented plainly.
	Glass, not noise
	Glassmorphic surfaces (translucent panels, soft blur, thin borders) are used sparingly for elevation — hero panels, modals, the live notification preview — never as a blanket texture across every card.
	Dark by default, light by necessity
	The dark-premium palette is the primary experience; light mode is not required for V1, keeping the design system's surface area smaller and more consistent.
	Mobile parity, not mobile compromise
	Every core workflow (compose, target, schedule, send) must be fully completable on a 375px viewport — mobile is a first-class authoring surface for this product's on-the-go publisher persona, not a read-only companion.
	AI is a suggestion, never a black box
	Every AI-generated output (titles, segments, timing, summaries) is visually distinct (a consistent AI badge/accent treatment) and always shows the human override path alongside it.
	2. Design System — Core Tokens
Full implementation-ready token values (hex codes, type scale, spacing scale) are specified in Section 21 — Design System Blueprint, written as a direct AI coding prompt. This section summarizes the rationale behind those tokens.
Token Group
	Direction
	Color
	Primary #3B82F6, secondary #60A5FA, accent #93C5FD form a single-hue blue ramp — chosen because blue reads as trustworthy/technical across all nine target audience segments and avoids the red/orange alert-fatigue risk of a more "energetic" palette for a notifications product. Dark background #0F172A and surface #111827 are deliberately distinct (not a single flat dark tone) so cards visibly separate from the page without relying on shadows alone.
	Typography
	A geometric sans for display/headings paired with a highly legible sans for body and data-table text (full pairing specified in Section 21) — optimized for the number-dense dashboard screens (KPIs, tables, charts) where legibility at small sizes matters more than personality.
	Spacing
	4px base grid throughout, so every component — from a button's internal padding to a dashboard's section gaps — aligns to the same rhythm, which is what makes a dense, data-heavy dashboard still feel calm rather than cluttered.
	Radius
	Small radii (4–8px) on functional elements (buttons, inputs, badges) and larger radii (12–16px) on containers (cards, modals) — reinforcing the 'glass panel' feel on containers while keeping interactive controls crisp.
	Shadow / Elevation
	On a dark background, elevation is communicated primarily through surface-color contrast and a 1px translucent border, with shadows used as a secondary, subtle cue — heavy drop shadows read poorly on dark UI and are avoided.

	5. Mobile Design Rules
* Touch targets: 44×44px minimum for every tappable element, including table-row action menus and chart legend toggles.
* Thumb zone: primary actions (Send, Save, Confirm) are placed in a sticky bottom bar or the bottom half of the viewport wherever a multi-step flow is involved (Campaign Builder, Onboarding Wizard); destructive actions are never placed where an accidental thumb-reach could trigger them.
* Navigation pattern: bottom tab bar (Home, Subscribers, Campaigns, Analytics, More) for the five highest-frequency dashboard destinations; a slide-out drawer from the 'More' tab houses Automation, Segments, AI Tools, Integration, Team, Billing, and Settings.
* Mobile keyboard behavior: numeric keyboards for numeric-only fields (e.g., max redemptions), email keyboards for email fields, and the live notification preview in Campaign Builder repositions above the keyboard rather than being obscured by it.
6. Desktop Design Rules
* Persistent left sidebar replaces bottom-tab navigation entirely above 1024px, exposing all eleven dashboard sections without a secondary 'More' menu.
* Hover states are meaningful, not decorative: table rows reveal their action menu on hover, chart series highlight on legend hover, and cards used as navigation (e.g., website switcher cards) lift 2px with a border-color shift.
* Keyboard shortcuts: '/' focuses the nearest search input, 'c' opens Campaign Builder from anywhere in the dashboard, 'Esc' closes the active modal/drawer — documented in a discoverable shortcuts-help overlay (triggered by '?').
* Data density: desktop tables show all relevant columns by default (vs. mobile's progressive disclosure into row-detail views) since desktop users are typically in an analysis or bulk-management mindset.
7. Animation & Motion
Motion philosophy: purposeful and subtle — motion confirms that an action registered (a campaign was sent, a toggle was flipped) rather than decorating the interface. Full timing-token values are specified in Section 21.
* Micro-interactions (button hover/press, toggle flip) use the fast duration tier for immediate-feeling feedback.
* Panel/modal/drawer entrances use the normal-to-slow tier with an ease-out curve so content feels like it is settling into place, not snapping.
* Scroll-triggered reveals on the marketing site (feature cards, stat counters) use Intersection Observer with a small staggered delay per grid item — applied only on the public marketing pages, never in the dashboard, where motion should stay minimal to avoid distracting from data review.
* All motion respects prefers-reduced-motion, falling back to instant state changes with no transition.
8. Accessibility Rules
* Focus ring: a consistent 2px outline in the accent blue with sufficient offset to remain visible against both dark surfaces and glassmorphic panels — never suppressed with outline:none without a custom replacement.
* ARIA labeling: icon-only buttons (table row actions, chart toggles) always carry an aria-label; charts expose a 'view as table' toggle as the primary accessible-data-access pattern rather than relying on chart-library auto-generated descriptions alone.
* Color-blind-safe check: the chart palette (used for CTR-by-campaign bars and delivery-by-browser donuts) is verified against deuteranopia/protanopia simulation, pairing color with a pattern or label rather than color alone for any state that conveys meaning (e.g., status badges always carry text, not just a colored dot).
* Reduced motion: every Framer Motion / CSS transition in the codebase is wrapped to check prefers-reduced-motion and substitutes an instant or near-instant equivalent.
9. Dark Mode
Dark is the only supported theme for V1 — there is no light-mode toggle. This is an explicit scope decision (see PRD Non-Goals) that reduces design-system surface area and matches the 'Dark Premium' brand direction every target persona will see from first contact via the marketing site through to daily dashboard use. Components that would typically need special dark-mode handling (e.g., code blocks in the Docs Hub, the rich-text editor in Blog CMS) are designed dark-native rather than adapted from a light baseline, avoiding any contrast or legibility regressions.

2. Entity Relationship Overview
workspaces is the tenant root: a workspace has many workspace_members (linking to users), many sites, one subscriptions row, many invoices, and many api_keys. Each site has many subscribers, segments, campaigns, and automations. Each subscriber has many subscriber_attributes. Each campaign has one campaign_stats row and many campaign_deliveries rows. Each automation has many automation_steps and many automation_runs (one per enrolled subscriber). blog_posts, audit_logs, and feature_flags are platform-level entities not scoped to a single workspace. plans and coupons are platform-level reference data consumed by subscriptions and the billing flow.

	6. Validation Rules
Field-Level
* Campaign title: required, 1–65 characters (push notification title display limit).
* Campaign body: required, 1–240 characters.
* Site domain: required, valid hostname format, must be unique within the owning workspace.
* Email fields (signup, team invite): RFC 5322 format validation, lowercase-normalized before storage.
* Segment rule_json: validated against a strict schema (allowed attributes, operators) server-side before save — the rule builder UI cannot be trusted to send only well-formed rules.
* API key scopes: validated against a fixed enum; unknown scope strings are rejected, not silently ignored.
Business Logic
* A campaign cannot transition to 'sending' status if the owning site's sending_enabled flag is false (admin-disabled) or the workspace's subscription status is 'past_due' beyond the grace period.
* A new site cannot be created if the workspace has already reached its plan's site_limit; the API returns a structured upgrade-prompt error the UI renders as the Billing upsell card.
* An automation cannot be activated with zero steps configured.
* A workspace cannot be deleted while it has an active (non-canceled) subscription; cancellation must complete first.

	2. Deep-Dive Analysis — Primary Competitors
OneSignal
* Hero Section: leads with the breadth claim ('one platform, every channel') rather than a single-channel promise — communicates scale and reduces perceived switching risk for growing teams.
* Navigation & IA: product-led mega-menu organized by channel (Push, In-App, Email, SMS) rather than by use case — clear but can overwhelm a visitor who only wants web push.
* Trust Architecture: published, calculable pricing (subscriber/MAU-based) is itself a trust signal in a category where many competitors hide pricing behind 'Contact Sales.'
* CTA Patterns: the free-tier CTA is consistently the most prominent button — the free plan is the actual primary conversion goal, not a secondary option.
* Conversion Flow: visitor → free signup → SDK install → first campaign, with documentation as the primary conversion-support content type.
* Mobile Experience: marketing site is fully responsive but the dashboard itself is desktop-oriented, reflecting the assumption that push management is a desk-based task — an assumption Prontly Notify deliberately rejects per its mobile-first dashboard mandate.
* Design Language: clean, blue-accented, light-mode-first — a visual space Prontly Notify's dark-premium direction deliberately avoids competing in directly.
* Content Strategy: documentation-heavy, developer-tone copy even on marketing pages — appropriate for OneSignal's broader enterprise buyer mix but heavier than the blogger/creator persona wants.
PushEngage
* Hero Section: leads with a concrete outcome claim (recovering lost visitors/sales) rather than a feature list — outcome-first messaging that resonates with the SMB/blogger buyer.
* Trust Architecture: case-study-driven, with specific recovered-revenue numbers per customer — strong bottom-of-funnel trust building for the e-commerce-leaning segment of its audience.
* CTA Patterns: pairs a low, explicit starting price directly next to the primary CTA, removing the 'how much will this cost me' hesitation at the point of decision.
* Conversion Flow: heavy use of pre-built automation templates (abandoned cart, win-back, price-drop) presented as one-click starting points rather than a blank canvas — directly informs Prontly Notify's Automation/Drip Builder template strategy.
3. UX Patterns To Adopt for Prontly Notify
Pattern: Calculable, published pricing with a usage slider
Inspired by: OneSignal. Implementation: the Pricing screen includes a subscriber-volume slider that recalculates the visible price live, removing any 'request a quote' friction for self-serve buyers. Why it works: pricing opacity is the single most-cited frustration in competitor reviews across this category.
Pattern: Pre-built automation templates as the default entry point
Inspired by: PushEngage, PushOwl. Implementation: the Automation/Drip Builder opens with a template gallery (Welcome Series, Re-engagement, Abandoned Content/Cart) before offering the blank-canvas flow editor. Why it works: blank-canvas automation builders have a well-documented activation-rate penalty versus template-first entry points.
Pattern: Outcome-first hero messaging, not feature-first
Inspired by: PushEngage. Implementation: Home hero headline leads with the re-engagement outcome ('Bring readers back without an email list') rather than the mechanism ('Browser push notification platform'). Why it works: the target personas (bloggers, e-commerce owners) buy outcomes, not technology categories.
Pattern: Revenue/engagement attribution tied directly to campaigns
Inspired by: PushOwl. Implementation: Analytics surfaces a 'conversions' column alongside CTR wherever a click_url maps to a tracked goal, not just raw delivery/click counts. Why it works: ROI-justification is the strongest retention lever for paid-tier accounts.
Pattern: First-class, documented REST API as a primary (not hidden) surface
Inspired by: WonderPush. Implementation: the Docs Hub gives the REST API equal navigational weight to the SDK install guides, and the API Keys screen is reachable from the main sidebar, not buried in account settings. Why it works: the developer/SaaS-founder persona actively screens out tools that treat the API as an afterthought.
Pattern: One-click, platform-native install flow
Inspired by: Webpushr. Implementation: the Website Onboarding Wizard's WordPress path links directly to a one-click plugin install rather than requiring manual code-paste for that platform specifically. Why it works: WordPress represents a large share of the blogger/publisher persona's installed base and one-click install measurably improves activation rate versus copy-paste snippets.
4. UX Anti-Patterns To Avoid
Anti-pattern: Gating basic automation behind the highest-priced tier
Seen on: several incumbent tools that reserve drip/journey automation for enterprise-only plans. Why to avoid: automation is the core differentiator Prontly Notify is positioning on; gating it away from the Starter tier would undercut the product's primary competitive wedge.
Anti-pattern: Desktop-only dashboard design
Seen on: OneSignal's dashboard, and most legacy competitors generally. Why to avoid: the blogger/creator persona frequently manages their site from a phone between other tasks; a desktop-only dashboard would silently exclude a meaningful share of the target audience from full product use.
Anti-pattern: 'Contact Sales' as the only path to see pricing past the entry tier
Seen on: several enterprise-leaning engagement platforms (CleverTap, Braze-tier competitors). Why to avoid: the self-serve, product-led growth motion this PRD specifies depends on a visitor being able to fully evaluate cost without a sales conversation.

	API Architecture
Full endpoint-by-endpoint detail is in the Backend Schema document and the AI Coding Prompt Package's API Architecture Prompt. This document covers the architectural decisions and conventions that bind the API together as a coherent system.
1. API Design Conventions
Convention
	Decision
	Base pattern
	/api/v1/[resource] — versioned from day one so the developer-persona's API-key integrations are never broken by an unannounced breaking change
	Response envelope
	{ success: boolean, data: any, error?: string, meta?: object } on every endpoint, including errors — clients never need to branch on HTTP status alone to determine response shape
	Pagination
	Cursor-based (not offset) on every list endpoint — required for the subscribers table specifically, where offset pagination degrades badly past tens of thousands of rows
	Auth methods
	Firebase ID token (dashboard, first-party) or a scoped API key with a Bearer prefix (developer/REST integrations) — both resolve to the same internal auth-context shape so route handlers don't need separate logic paths
	Idempotency
	Write endpoints that could plausibly be retried by a client (campaign send, subscriber export trigger) accept an optional Idempotency-Key header to prevent duplicate side effects on network-retry scenarios
	2. Internal vs. Public API Surface
Internal (dashboard-only) endpoints: Most of the surface — Sites, Subscribers, Segments, Campaigns, Automations, AI, Analytics, Team, Billing — are callable by the dashboard's own session and, where explicitly scoped, by a developer's API key. There is no separate 'internal API' the dashboard uses that customers cannot also access; the dashboard is simply the first, most fully-featured client of the same public API surface — a deliberate decision that keeps the API honest and complete rather than allowing it to drift behind dashboard-only shortcuts.
Admin (staff-only) endpoints: /api/v1/admin/* is a genuinely separate, staff-authenticated surface, never exposed to customer-issued API keys under any scope, since it operates across tenant boundaries by design.
3. Middleware Stack (applied in order to every request)
1. CORS — restricts cross-origin calls to the marketing/dashboard origin for session-based requests; API-key-authenticated requests are allowed broader CORS since they're meant for server-to-server or client-side SDK use from the customer's own domain.
2. Rate limiting — see Security Architecture for tier-specific limits.
3. Request logging — structured logs (request ID, route, auth context, latency) shipped to the hosting provider's log aggregation for observability.
4. Auth verification — resolves Firebase ID token or API key into a consistent { userId | apiKeyId, workspaceId, role, scopes } context object.
5. Input sanitization — strips/escapes any HTML in free-text fields (campaign body, segment names) before validation, defending against stored-XSS even if a later rendering path forgets to escape output.
6. Zod schema validation — endpoint-specific, rejects malformed payloads before any database write is attempted.
7. Response formatting — wraps the handler's return value in the standard envelope.
4. Webhook Architecture
Webhook
	Source
	Handling
	Razorpay subscription/payment events
	Razorpay
	Signature-verified using the webhook secret; idempotent processing keyed on Razorpay's event ID to safely handle redelivery
	Outbound: campaign delivery events (future roadmap)
	Prontly Notify → customer's own endpoint
	Planned for the Scale/Enterprise tier, allowing customers to pipe delivery/click events into their own CRM or analytics stack
	5. Why This Architecture Fits a Multi-Tenant, AI-Augmented Push Platform
The single-envelope, cursor-paginated, dual-auth-method design keeps the API simple enough for a small founding engineering team to maintain while remaining genuinely usable by the developer persona as a first-class integration surface — a gap several competitors leave unaddressed by treating their API as a secondary, dashboard-mirroring afterthought.
Security Architecture
Full implementation-level detail (headers, rate-limit numbers, hashing parameters) is in the AI Coding Prompt Package's Security Implementation Prompt. This document covers the threat model and architectural reasoning specific to a multi-tenant push notification platform.
1. Threat Model — What Makes This Product's Security Posture Distinct
Threat
	Why It's Elevated For This Product
	Architectural Response
	Sender-reputation abuse (spam/phishing via push)
	A single abusive tenant sending unsolicited or deceptive notifications can damage FCM/browser-vendor trust for the entire platform, affecting every other tenant's deliverability
	Automated content screening on campaign send, the Website Oversight admin module, and a sending_enabled kill-switch enforced server-side regardless of UI state
	Cross-tenant data leakage
	Every query (subscribers, campaigns, segments) must be scoped to the requesting workspace; a single missing WHERE clause could expose one customer's subscriber data to another
	Every D1 query in the data-access layer is generated through a scoped query builder that requires a workspace/site ID parameter — there is no raw-query code path that bypasses this scoping in application code
	API key compromise
	A leaked customer API key could be used to exfiltrate subscriber data or send unauthorized campaigns from a legitimate tenant's account
	Scoped permissions per key (read-only keys cannot send), key-prefix-only display after creation, and per-key usage monitoring on the API Keys screen so customers can self-detect anomalous use
	Admin impersonation misuse
	Staff impersonation is operationally necessary for support but is also the highest-leverage internal-abuse vector
	Every impersonation session is time-boxed, audit-logged, and visually disclosed to the impersonated account's owner via an activity-log entry they can see
	2. Authentication & Session Security
* Firebase Authentication handles credential storage and password hashing — Prontly Notify never stores raw or self-hashed passwords, eliminating an entire class of credential-storage risk from the application's own threat surface.
* ID tokens are short-lived; the Firebase client SDK handles silent refresh, and Next.js middleware re-verifies the token signature on every protected request rather than trusting a cached session flag.
* 2FA (TOTP) is available from Account Settings and is presented as a recommended (not yet mandatory for V1) step during onboarding for Owner-role accounts specifically, given their elevated blast radius (billing, team, API keys).
3. Data Protection
* Subscriber FCM tokens are treated as sensitive identifiers: never returned in any list/export response in raw form to the dashboard UI beyond what's needed for the Subscribers table display, and excluded entirely from CSV exports unless explicitly requested with an additional confirmation step.
* Site-level VAPID private keys are encrypted at the application layer (AES-256-GCM) before being written to D1, with the encryption key held in a hosting-provider-managed secret store, not in the application's own database.
* PII minimization: IP addresses used for geo-resolution at subscribe time are resolved to country/city and then discarded — raw IPs are never persisted, reducing both breach impact and DPDPA/GDPR compliance scope.
4. Incident Response Posture
1. Delivery Monitoring (admin) provides the first detection layer for both reliability and abuse-pattern incidents.
2. A confirmed incident triggers an Audit Logs entry and, for customer-impacting events, a notice surfaced via the affected workspace's notification preferences.
3. Post-incident, the Audit Logs and Delivery Monitoring error logs together provide the forensic record for a written post-mortem — required practice before closing any Severity 1/2 incident.

	Scalability Blueprint
1. Scaling Dimensions Specific to This Product
Dimension
	What Drives Growth
	Scaling Approach
	Subscriber volume per site
	Successful publisher/store growth
	subscribers table is indexed and queried strictly per-site; large-site CSV export and segment-count operations run as async jobs rather than blocking requests, so a single very large site cannot degrade response times for other tenants

	2. Known Scaling Ceilings & Trigger Points for Re-architecture
* D1 database/table size: monitor against Cloudflare's documented D1 limits; the trigger point for introducing per-large-tenant database sharding (one D1 database per very-high-volume site) is when any single site's subscribers table approaches a meaningful fraction of D1's per-database size ceiling — flagged as an Open Question in the TRD pending a load-test validation pass.
* campaign_deliveries table growth: the 90-day retention/pruning policy (see Backend Schema) is the primary control; if analytics-retention requirements grow beyond 90 days, the next step is rolling older records into a compressed, R2-stored archive rather than keeping them in D1 indefinitely.
* Vercel serverless function concurrency: monitor function invocation/duration metrics; the AI endpoints (LLM-call-bound, higher latency) are the most likely first bottleneck and are already isolated into their own route group so they can be moved to a dedicated longer-timeout configuration without affecting the rest of the API.
3. Horizontal Scaling Readiness
Because the application layer is entirely stateless serverless functions (no in-process session state, no sticky-session requirements), horizontal scaling of compute is handled automatically by Vercel's platform with no architectural changes required as request volume grows. The primary engineering investment as the product scales is therefore concentrated in the data layer (D1 partitioning strategy) and the delivery pipeline (FCM batch-send throughput), not in the request-handling layer.
4. Multi-Region Considerations
Cloudflare's edge network already serves R2 assets and D1 reads close to the requesting user globally without additional configuration, which is a meaningful advantage for the platform's stated US/UK/Canada/Australia secondary markets alongside its India-primary base. Write-heavy paths (campaign sends, subscriber ingestion) remain centralized through D1's primary-write-location model for V1; multi-region write distribution is not required at the current target scale and is deferred as a post-Series-A consideration.

Database Schema Prompt
DATABASE SCHEMA PROMPT
 
Design and implement the complete database schema for Prontly Notify.
Database: Cloudflare D1 (serverless SQLite, edge-replicated)
 
Entity: users — Purpose: one row per human login (customers + internal staff)
  Fields: id (TEXT/UUID, PK), firebase_uid (TEXT, unique), email (TEXT, unique),
  name, avatar_url, email_verified (BOOL), is_staff (BOOL), staff_role (TEXT,
  nullable), notification_prefs (JSON), last_login_at, created_at, updated_at
  Indexes: UNIQUE(firebase_uid), UNIQUE(email)
  Security: row readable/writable only by its own user; staff rows additionally
  readable by other staff
 
Entity: workspaces — Purpose: the billing/tenant root entity
  Fields: id (PK), owner_user_id (FK users), name, plan_id (FK plans),
  razorpay_customer_id, default_timezone, created_at, updated_at
  Indexes: INDEX(owner_user_id)  |  Relationships: 1 workspace → many sites,
  workspace_members, invoices, api_keys; 1 workspace → 1 subscriptions row
  Security: Owner/Admin (team role) full access; Member read-only, non-billing fields
 
Entity: workspace_members — Purpose: team membership + role within a workspace
  Fields: id (PK), workspace_id (FK), user_id (FK, nullable until accepted),
  invited_email, role ('owner'|'admin'|'member'), site_access (JSON: 'all' or
  site-ID array), status ('active'|'pending'), invited_at, joined_at
  Indexes: INDEX(workspace_id), INDEX(user_id)
 
Entity: sites — Purpose: a registered website with its own FCM/VAPID credentials
  Fields: id (PK), workspace_id (FK), name, domain, category, platform,
  vapid_public_key, vapid_private_key (AES-256-GCM encrypted at write time),
  install_status ('pending'|'verified'|'broken'), onboarding_step (0-3),
  sending_enabled (BOOL, default true), created_at, updated_at
  Indexes: INDEX(workspace_id), UNIQUE(domain, workspace_id)
 
Entity: subscribers — Purpose: one row per browser push subscription (highest-volume table)
  Fields: id (PK), site_id (FK), fcm_token (unique per site), browser, os,
  country, city, status ('active'|'unsubscribed'), subscribed_at, last_seen_at,
  unsubscribed_at
  Indexes: INDEX(site_id, status), UNIQUE(site_id, fcm_token),
  INDEX(site_id, last_seen_at)
  Security: never returned in raw token form beyond what the Subscribers table needs
 
Entity: subscriber_attributes — Purpose: flexible key-value tags per subscriber
  Fields: id (PK), subscriber_id (FK), key (max 64 chars), value (max 256 chars),
  updated_at  |  Indexes: INDEX(subscriber_id), INDEX(key, value)
 
Entity: segments — Purpose: saved/dynamic audience definitions
  Fields: id (PK), site_id (FK), name, type ('manual'|'dynamic'|'ai_suggested'),
  rule_json, subscriber_count_cached (INT), created_by_user_id (FK, nullable),
  created_at, updated_at  |  Indexes: INDEX(site_id)
 
Entity: campaigns — Purpose: a single push notification campaign
  Fields: id (PK), site_id (FK), title (max 65 chars), body (max 240 chars),
  icon_url, image_url, click_url, action_buttons (JSON, max 2), segment_id (FK,
  nullable = all subscribers), status ('draft'|'scheduled'|'sending'|'sent'|'failed'),
  scheduled_at, sent_at, ai_generated (BOOL), created_by_user_id, created_at, updated_at
  Indexes: INDEX(site_id, status), INDEX(site_id, scheduled_at)
 
Entity: campaign_stats — Purpose: pre-aggregated per-campaign counters
  Fields: campaign_id (PK, FK), sent_count, delivered_count, failed_count,
  click_count (all INT, default 0, atomically incremented), updated_at
 
Entity: campaign_deliveries — Purpose: per-subscriber delivery record (90-day retention)
  Fields: id (PK), campaign_id (FK), subscriber_id (FK), status
  ('pending'|'delivered'|'failed'|'clicked'), error_code, sent_at, delivered_at,
  clicked_at  |  Indexes: INDEX(campaign_id, status)
 
Entity: automations — Fields: id (PK), site_id (FK), name, trigger_type
  ('new_subscriber'|'tag_added'|'page_visited'|'inactive_days'|'ai_suggested'),
  trigger_config (JSON), status ('active'|'paused'|'draft'), created_at, updated_at
 
Entity: automation_steps — Fields: id (PK), automation_id (FK), step_order (INT),
  type ('wait'|'send'|'condition'), config (JSON)
  Indexes: INDEX(automation_id, step_order)
 
Entity: automation_runs — Purpose: per-subscriber progress through an automation
  Fields: id (PK), automation_id (FK), subscriber_id (FK), current_step_id
  (FK, nullable), status ('active'|'completed'|'exited'), next_action_at,
  started_at, completed_at  |  Indexes: INDEX(status, next_action_at) — the
  primary index the Cron worker scans every tick
 
Entity: plans — Fields: id (PK, e.g. 'free'/'growth'), name, price_monthly,
  price_annual (INT, paise), subscriber_limit, site_limit, ai_credit_limit,
  team_seat_limit (INT, -1 = unlimited), features (JSON)
 
Entity: subscriptions — Fields: id (PK), workspace_id (FK, unique), plan_id (FK),
  razorpay_subscription_id (unique, nullable), status ('active'|'past_due'|
  'canceled'|'trialing'), current_period_start, current_period_end, created_at, updated_at

	API Architecture Prompt
API ARCHITECTURE PROMPT
 
Design and implement the complete API layer for Prontly Notify.
 
API Structure:
- Base URL pattern: /api/v1/[resource]
- Auth: Firebase ID token (Bearer, dashboard/first-party) OR scoped API key
  (Bearer, developer/REST integrations) — both resolve to one auth-context shape
- Response format: { success: boolean, data: any, error?: string, meta?: object }
- Pagination: cursor-based on every list endpoint (never offset-based)
 
Resource: sites
  GET    /api/v1/sites              — List (workspace-scoped)
  POST   /api/v1/sites              — Create (enforces plan site_limit)
  GET    /api/v1/sites/:id          — Get single
  PATCH  /api/v1/sites/:id          — Update
  DELETE /api/v1/sites/:id          — Delete
  Auth: Authenticated, scoped to workspace_members.site_access
  Validation: Zod — domain format, unique-per-workspace
  Rate Limiting: 100 req/min per workspace
 
Resource: subscribers
  POST /api/v1/sites/:id/subscribers          — Public (site-key auth, SDK-originated)
  GET  /api/v1/sites/:id/subscribers           — Authenticated, scoped, paginated, filterable
  POST /api/v1/sites/:id/subscribers/export    — Authenticated, scoped, async job
  Rate Limiting: ingestion endpoint 60 req/min per site (anti-poisoning)
 
Resource: segments / campaigns / automations
  Standard GET (list) / POST (create) / GET :id / PATCH :id / DELETE :id pattern
  Plus: POST /campaigns/:id/send, POST /campaigns/:id/duplicate,
  POST /segments/:id/preview-count
  Auth: Authenticated, scoped  |  Validation: title/body length limits, rule_json
  schema enforcement  |  Rate Limiting: 100 req/min per workspace
 
Resource: ai
  POST /api/v1/ai/generate-title, /optimize-ctr, /suggest-send-time,
  /campaign-recommendations  |  GET /api/v1/ai/suggest-segments,
  /analytics-summary, /usage
  Auth: Authenticated, scoped, AI-credit metered against ai_usage
  Rate Limiting: 20 req/min per workspace (LLM-call-bound, higher cost per request)
 
Resource: billing
  GET /api/v1/billing/plans (public), GET /subscription, POST /change-plan,
  POST /cancel, POST /webhook (Razorpay signature-verified)
  Auth: Owner/Admin role only (except plans + webhook)
 
Resource: admin (staff-only, never reachable via customer API keys)
  GET /api/v1/admin/overview, /accounts, /sites; POST /accounts/:id/suspend,
  /accounts/:id/impersonate, /sites/:id/disable-sending
  GET/PATCH /api/v1/admin/audit-logs, /feature-flags (super_admin only)
  Auth: Staff is_staff=true, staff_role checked per-route
 
Global Middleware Stack (applied in order):
1. CORS configuration — session requests restricted to app origin; API-key
   requests allowed broader CORS for customer-side SDK use
2. Rate limiting — sliding window, tiered per resource as specified above
3. Request logging — structured logs (request ID, route, auth context, latency)
4. Auth verification — resolves Firebase token or API key into
   { userId|apiKeyId, workspaceId, role, scopes }
5. Input sanitization — strip/escape HTML in free-text fields before validation
6. Zod schema validation — endpoint-specific, rejects malformed payloads
7. Response formatting — wraps handler output in the standard envelope

	Security Implementation Prompt
SECURITY IMPLEMENTATION PROMPT
 
Implement a complete security layer for Prontly Notify.
Security Priority: High (multi-tenant SaaS handling subscriber PII + payment data)
 
1. API Security
   - Firebase ID token validation on every protected route via Next.js middleware
   - Token lifetime managed by Firebase SDK (short-lived ID token, silent
     refresh) — no custom JWT issuance to maintain
   - Scoped API keys for developer integrations: read vs. write scopes enforced
     per endpoint; revoked keys rejected immediately (checked on every request,
     not cached)
 
2. Input Validation & Sanitization
   - Validate all inputs server-side with Zod — never trust client-side
     validation alone
   - Sanitize HTML in campaign body, segment names, and blog post content to
     prevent stored XSS
   - Parameterized queries only via the scoped D1 query-builder layer — no
     string-concatenated SQL anywhere in the codebase
   - File upload validation (R2 uploads): enforce content-type allowlist
     (image/png, image/webp, image/jpeg) and a max file size (2MB for
     notification icons, 5MB for blog images)
 
3. CSRF Protection
   - SameSite=Strict on the session cookie
   - Double-submit CSRF token on every dashboard form POST/PATCH/DELETE
   - Origin header validation on state-changing requests as a second layer
 
4. Rate Limiting
   - Auth endpoints (login, signup, forgot-password): 5 requests / 15 minutes per IP
   - Subscriber ingestion endpoint: 60 requests / minute per site
   - Authenticated API endpoints: 100 requests / minute per workspace
   - AI endpoints: 20 requests / minute per workspace (LLM-cost-aware limit)
   - Public marketing-site endpoints (contact form, newsletter): 10 requests /
     minute per IP
   - Return 429 with a Retry-After header on every limit
 
5. HTTP Security Headers
   - Content-Security-Policy scoped to Firebase, Razorpay Checkout, GA4, and
     the application's own origin
   - X-Frame-Options: DENY
   - X-Content-Type-Options: nosniff
   - Strict-Transport-Security: max-age=63072000; includeSubDomains; preload
   - Referrer-Policy: strict-origin-when-cross-origin
 
6. Authentication Security
   - Password hashing fully delegated to Firebase Authentication (no
     application-managed password storage)
   - Brute-force lockout behavior inherited from Firebase Auth's built-in
     anomaly detection, supplemented by the rate limits above
   - 2FA support (TOTP) available from Account Settings, recommended at
     onboarding for Owner-role accounts
 
[High security priority — additionally implemented]:
   - Full audit logging on every staff/admin mutation (see audit_logs schema)
   - Encryption at the application layer (AES-256-GCM) for site-level VAPID
     private keys before any database write
   - A documented penetration-test checklist executed before the Production
     Launch Checklist is signed off (auth bypass attempts, cross-tenant data
     access attempts, rate-limit evasion, file-upload exploitation)

	Addresses the three most common objections directly: billing cycle/cancellation, data export on downgrade, and whether the Free plan is genuinely usable in production
	5. Urgency & Scarcity
Used only where genuinely applicable — manufactured urgency would directly contradict the brand's 'trustworthy, premium, technical' positioning and the Content Strategy's explicit no-artificial-urgency tone guideline.
* Waitlist/early access: not applicable — the product is self-serve and immediately available, no gated launch.
* Limited seats/licenses: not applicable to V1's open self-serve model.
* Time-limited offer: reserved exclusively for genuine, dated promotional coupon campaigns (see Coupons & Discounts admin module) — always shows a real, enforced expiry date, never a rolling or fake countdown.
* Countdown timers: not used anywhere in the core product experience; the only legitimate use is an active, admin-created coupon's actual expiry, surfaced as a plain date, not a ticking clock.

	Production Launch Checklist
A consolidated, sign-off-ready checklist pulled from across this document — Deployment Architecture, the Deployment Prompt, Security Architecture, and the SEO Blueprint — organized as the literal sequence to execute in the final week before Prontly Notify goes live.
Infrastructure & Environment
* All environment variables from the Project Architecture Prompt are set in Vercel's Production environment (cross-checked line-by-line against the documented list, not from memory).
* Cloudflare D1 production database created, migrated, and seeded with the plans table.
* Cloudflare R2 production buckets created with the correct public/private access rules per the Storage Architecture table.
* Production domain DNS configured and verified pointing at Vercel; SSL certificate active and auto-renewing.
* Firebase project switched to its production configuration (not the development/test project used during the build).
* FCM production service-account credentials configured — confirmed distinct from any development/test FCM credentials.
* Razorpay account switched from test mode to live mode; live webhook URL registered and signature secret updated in production environment variables.
Data & Migrations
* All D1 migrations have been run against production and verified with a row-count sanity check per table.
* A manual database backup/export has been taken and a restore has been test-run at least once before go-live.
* No development/test/seed data (synthetic subscribers, demo campaigns) exists in the production database.
Security
* Security headers (CSP, HSTS, X-Frame-Options, X-Content-Type-Options, Referrer-Policy) verified live via an external header-scan tool.
* Rate limiting confirmed active on auth and subscriber-ingestion endpoints with a real test request hitting the configured limit.
* CSRF protection confirmed active on at least one real dashboard form submission in production.
* Audit logging confirmed writing a real row for a test admin action performed against production (then reviewed and noted as a test in the log).
* VAPID private key encryption confirmed active — a freshly created production site's stored key is verified to be ciphertext, not plaintext, via direct database inspection.
SEO & Discoverability
* robots.txt verified live at the production URL, correctly disallowing /dashboard/, /admin/, and /api/.
* sitemap.xml verified live and includes all marketing pages, published blog posts, and Docs Hub platform pages.
* Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools connected and the production sitemap submitted.
* Schema.org markup validated with zero errors via Google's Rich Results Test on Home, Pricing, and one Blog post.
* GA4 tracking confirmed firing on a real production pageview and a real signup-completion test event.
Monitoring & Observability
* Sentry confirmed capturing both a deliberately triggered client-side error and a deliberately triggered server-side exception, each with a readable source-mapped stack trace.
* Vercel Analytics confirmed collecting Core Web Vitals data on production traffic.
* Uptime monitoring (BetterStack/UptimeRobot or equivalent) configured against the marketing site and an authenticated API health endpoint, with alerting routed to the team.
* Delivery Monitoring's FCM success-rate and queue-depth metrics confirmed populating with real data from a real test campaign send.

Legal & Compliance
* Privacy Policy, Terms of Service, Cookie Policy, and Refund Policy are live, dated, and linked from the Signup screen's required-acceptance checkbox and the footer.
* Cookie consent banner confirmed blocking GA4 cookies until explicit consent is given.

Team Readiness
* The incident-response runbook from Phase 10 is written, reviewed, and accessible to every team member who could be on call.
* At least one team member has walked through the Admin Panel's moderation tools (Suspend, Disable Sending, Impersonate) in a staging environment before needing to use them live under pressure.

SaaS Scaling Roadmap
Post-launch operating roadmap across four growth stages. Each stage lists the trigger that signals it's time to move forward, the technical/infrastructure work due, the team/process changes due, and the product priorities for that stage.
Stage 0 — Launch to First 100 Paying Accounts
Dimension
	Focus
	Trigger
	Production launch (Production Launch Checklist complete)
	Infrastructure
	Default Vercel/D1/R2 configuration is sufficient with no changes needed; focus engineering time on bug-fixing real usage patterns surfaced by Sentry, not premature scaling work
	Team
	Founding team handles support, content, and engineering directly; no dedicated support hire yet
	Product Priority
	Activation-rate optimization (time-to-first-verified-install, time-to-first-campaign-sent) — the single highest-leverage metric at this stage, per the PRD's Success Metrics
	Watch For
	Any pattern of onboarding drop-off at a specific wizard step (instrumented via the App Flow's universal state model) — the first sign of a friction point worth fixing immediately
	Stage 1 — 100 to 1,000 Paying Accounts
Dimension
	Focus
	Trigger
	Consistent month-over-month signup growth and the first signs of D1 query latency creeping up on the largest 1-2 customer sites
	Infrastructure
	Validate the D1 per-database size ceiling against real largest-tenant data (the Open Question flagged in the TRD); implement the campaign_deliveries 90-day pruning job if not already running; begin tracking Vercel function cold-start/duration metrics specifically for the AI-endpoint route group
	Team
	First dedicated support/success hire to handle the growing support-ticket volume the founding team can no longer absorb alongside building; consider a part-time content writer to sustain the Content Strategy's publishing cadence
	Product Priority
	Expand the Automation template library based on real usage data (which templates get used vs. ignored); begin the formal referral program (flagged as post-V1 in the Conversion Strategy Report)
	Watch For
	Free-to-paid conversion rate trend — if it's flat or declining as volume grows, revisit the Monetization Blueprint's upgrade-trigger copy and timing before assuming a pricing problem
	Stage 2 — 1,000 to 10,000 Paying Accounts
Dimension
	Focus
	Trigger
	A specific large tenant's subscriber table approaches a meaningful fraction of D1's documented per-database size limit, or aggregate platform-wide send volume starts pushing FCM batch-send throughput limits
	Infrastructure
	Implement per-large-tenant D1 database sharding for the small number of outlier high-volume sites (the architecture anticipates this — see Scalability Blueprint — rather than requiring a rewrite); move campaign_deliveries archival from simple pruning to a compressed R2-archive-and-purge pipeline; introduce a dedicated, longer-timeout Vercel configuration for the AI endpoint route group if latency has become customer-visible
	Team
	Build a small dedicated platform/infra function distinct from product engineering; formalize an on-call rotation beyond the single-owner model from Stage 0/1
	Product Priority
	Begin Enterprise-tier productization: SSO, custom SLA terms, and a dedicated onboarding flow for larger agency/enterprise accounts (explicitly deferred from V1 scope in the PRD's Non-Goals)
	Watch For
	Sender-reputation health platform-wide (Delivery Monitoring's FCM success rate) — at this scale a single abusive tenant has materially more potential to affect every other tenant's deliverability; the Website Oversight moderation tooling becomes a daily-use tool, not an occasional one
	Stage 3 — 10,000+ Paying Accounts / Series A+ Scale
Dimension
	Focus
	Trigger
	Platform-wide scale where multi-region write distribution, dedicated compliance tooling, and a formal enterprise sales motion all become justified investments
	Infrastructure
	Evaluate multi-region D1/write-path distribution for the secondary US/UK/Canada/Australia markets if latency data justifies it; evaluate whether the LLM provider relationship should move from pay-per-call to a negotiated volume contract given AI feature usage at this scale
	Team
	Dedicated security/compliance function to support enterprise procurement reviews (SOC 2 readiness becomes a realistic and likely necessary investment at this stage, explicitly flagged as out of scope for V1 in the PRD's Anti-Persona)
	Product Priority
	Native mobile push (iOS/Android SDKs) and full omnichannel orchestration (SMS, WhatsApp, in-app) — both explicitly deferred in the PRD's Non-Goals for V1 — become reasonable roadmap items once the core web-push product has proven product-market fit and the team/infrastructure investment is justified by scale
	Watch For
	Net revenue retention trend — at this scale, expansion revenue from existing accounts should be doing more of the growth work than new-logo acquisition; if it isn't, revisit the Monetization Blueprint's expansion-revenue mechanisms before investing further in top-of-funnel acquisition spend
	Cross-Stage Principles
* Never scale infrastructure ahead of a confirmed, measured trigger — every stage above is gated by a real signal (latency, table size, support volume), not a calendar date or a vanity metric.
* Every infrastructure investment at each stage should be traceable back to a specific Open Question or scaling ceiling already flagged in the TRD or Scalability Blueprint, not a net-new architectural decision made under growth pressure.
* Team scaling follows revenue and operational load, not the reverse — the founding team should feel the specific pain (support backlog, on-call fatigue, a stalled enterprise deal) that a new hire or system resolves before making that hire or building that system.
